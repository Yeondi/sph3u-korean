Lab activity: Force, mass, and acceleration accessible version

Purpose

The purpose of this experiment is to:

  1. Determine the relationship between acceleration and net force,
  2. Determine the relationship between acceleration and mass.

Materials

This experiment uses a computer simulation. For this accessible version of the lab activity, observations are presented in the tables that follow.

Apparatus

The simulated apparatus consists of a wagon on a frictionless surface, attached to a hanging mass by a string that rests on a frictionless pulley. The mass of the wagon and hanging block can be adjusted using sliders. The distance the wagon moves is 1 metre.

Method

There are two experiments related to the two purposes of this experiment.

Experiment 1

  1. Set the mass of the wagon to 100 gram (0.10 kg) and the hanging mass to 1 gram (0.001 kg).
  2. Press the Start button and observe the movement of the wagon and hanging mass. Record observations in the results tables.
  3. Repeat the previous two steps three (3) more times, but change the value of the hanging mass to 2.0 grams, 4.0 grams, and 6.0 grams.

Experiment 2

  1. With the hanging mass set at 1 gram and the wagon set at 100 grams, run the experiment and record the data in the results table.
  2. Repeat step 1 three more times, but change the mass of the wagon to 120 grams, 140 grams, and 160 grams.

Analysis

  1. Since the wagon and the hanging mass are attached together, they experience the same movement. They travel the same distance in the same amount of time. Both objects seem to be speeding up. If you can find the acceleration of one object, then you will know the acceleration of the other. You already know the displacement travelled by the wagon (1 metre) and the time this took, so you can use the kinematics equation d = v 1 t + 1 2 a t 2 to find the acceleration. Both the wagon and the hanging mass have zero initial velocity, since the system was initially at rest. Rearrange the equation to solve for acceleration, noting that initial velocity is zero and distance is 1 metre. The equation then becomes a = 2   metres t 2 to calculate acceleration. You do not need to include direction, as you are only interested in the magnitude. Calculate the acceleration of each cart, in each of the eight trials.
  2. The force of gravity (weight) of an object is calculated by multiplying the mass by g, acceleration due to gravity, which is 9.8 newtons per kilogram near the surface of the Earth. Calculate the net force on the hanging mass, for each of the eight trials.
  3. Using graph paper or computer software, referring to Experiment 1’s data, create a scatter plot of acceleration (on the vertical axis) versus force of gravity (on the horizontal axis).
  4. Using computer software or manual work, create a line of best fit for your graph and find its slope, with units.
  5. Comment on the relationship between acceleration and net force in Experiment 1. How is this related to the slope of the line of best fit?
  6. Using graph paper or computer software, referring to Experiment 2’s data, create a scatter plot of acceleration (on the vertical axis) versus the mass of the wagon (on the horizontal axis).
  7. Describe the relationship between acceleration and the mass of the wagon.
  8. For each trial in Experiment 2, calculate the reciprocal of the wagon’s mass (one divided by the wagon’s mass). Using graph paper or computer software, referring to Experiment 2’s data including this new value, create a scatter plot of acceleration (on the vertical axis) versus the reciprocal of the wagon’s mass (on the horizontal axis).
  9. Using computer software or manual work, create a line of best fit for your graph and find its slope, with units.
  10. Comment on the relationship between acceleration and the reciprocal of mass in Experiment 2. How is this related to the slope of the line of best fit?
